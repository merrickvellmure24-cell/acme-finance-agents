## Concept

A **budget** is what each department was approved to spend. **Variance** is the gap between that plan and reality.

- **Over budget (unfavorable variance)** = spent more than approved
- **Under budget (favorable variance)** = spent less than approved

FP&A (Financial Planning & Analysis) owns this process. The budget is set annually or quarterly; actuals come from accounting systems monthly.

## Acme example

Acme tracks **8 departments × 6 months** of planned vs actual:

Notable variances in the demo data:
- **Marketing/Events** — MODEX conference $45K overage + ProMat $32K overage = major unfavorable variance
- Other departments may show smaller over/under patterns

The budget agent calculates:
- Dollar variance per department per month
- Percentage over/under
- Projected year-end totals if trends continue

## So what?

Budget variance answers **"who is driving the burn?"** — cash position tells you *how much* you spend; budget tells you *where*.

| Variance pattern | CFO action |
|-----------------|-----------|
| One-time overage (conference) | Approve if ROI justified; cap next year |
| Persistent overrun (3+ months) | Meeting with department head, reforecast |
| Under budget | Don't automatically reallocate — understand why |
| Company-wide overrun | Board-level conversation about burn guidance |

At Acme, the conference overages are a **one-time event** that still impacts runway. The CFO would:
1. Require pre-approval for future conference spend > $20K
2. Ask Marketing for pipeline ROI from MODEX/ProMat
3. Adjust Q3 budget allocation to compensate

## If they ask…

> **"Is being under budget always good?"**

"No. Chronic underspend in Engineering might mean delayed hiring that hurts product delivery. Underspend in Sales might mean missed revenue targets. Context matters — variance is a signal, not a scorecard."

> **"How does this connect to runway?"**

"Every dollar of unfavorable variance accelerates burn, which shortens runway. The budget tab explains *why* burn is $1.34M instead of $1.0M — it's the decomposition behind the cash position headline number."
