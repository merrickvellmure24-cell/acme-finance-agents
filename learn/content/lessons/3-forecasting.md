## Concept

A **cash forecast** projects your bank balance into the future based on assumptions about spending and revenue. It's the "what happens if we do nothing?" answer.

Acme models **three scenarios** over 9 months:

| Scenario | Assumption | Purpose |
|----------|-----------|---------|
| **Current trajectory** | Burn and revenue continue as-is | Baseline — worst case if no action |
| **Spending discipline** | Burn reduced toward guidance | Shows runway extension from cost control |
| **Revenue growth** | Revenue increases, burn moderates | Shows upside if sales deliver |

## Acme example

Starting from $12.68M cash at ~$1.34M/month burn:
- **Current trajectory** — runway exhausts in roughly 9–10 months
- **Spending discipline** — cutting toward $1.0M/mo extends runway by several months
- **Revenue growth** — new bookings improve the curve further

The forecast doesn't predict the future perfectly. It **frames choices** for the board: cut costs, grow revenue, raise capital, or some combination.

## So what?

Forecasts drive three critical decisions:

1. **Fundraising timing** — if current trajectory hits critical cash in Q1, you need to start a raise in Q3 (raises take 3–6 months)
2. **Cost cut magnitude** — spending discipline scenario shows how much you need to cut to reach 18-month runway
3. **Revenue plan credibility** — growth scenario only matters if sales pipeline supports it

A CFO presents all three scenarios to the board, not just the optimistic one. The current trajectory is the anchor.

- **Conclusion cards** → Agent identifies the month cash becomes critical
- Cross-reference with **KPI Bar runway** — forecast should be directionally consistent

## Try it

1. Go to **Cash Forecast** in the sidebar
2. Identify where the **current trajectory** line drops below $2M (typical minimum operating buffer)
3. Compare the gap between current trajectory and spending discipline — that's the "cost of inaction"
4. Read the agent's RED/YELLOW conclusions about critical months
5. Ask yourself: which scenario would you present to a board?

## If they ask…

> **"Why three scenarios instead of one forecast?"**

"Single-point forecasts create false confidence. Three scenarios bracket reality — the baseline shows risk if nothing changes, discipline shows the cost-cut path, and growth shows upside if execution delivers. Boards expect this range, not a single number."

> **"How is this different from a budget?"**

"Budget is planned spend by department — backward-looking accountability. Cash forecast is forward-looking survival math — it combines burn, revenue, and timing to answer 'when do we run out of money?' They complement each other."
